Study 7

When Grace Becomes a Ceiling

Using forgiveness as a reason not to fight sin

"What shall we say then? Are we to continue in sin that grace may abound? By no means! How can we who died to sin still live in it?"

Romans 6:1–2 (ESV)

"For certain people have crept in unnoticed who long ago were designated for this condemnation, ungodly people, who pervert the grace of our God into sensuality and deny our only Master and Lord, Jesus Christ."

Jude 4 (ESV)

Paul anticipated this question. He knew that if you preach grace without qualification — the grace that justifies the ungodly, that is given not because of works but because of faith — someone will ask: doesn't that mean we can keep sinning? He knew it well enough to interrupt his own argument in Romans 6 with the question, and then to spend the entire chapter answering it. The answer is not a qualification of grace. It is a clarification of what grace actually does.

The Antinomian Trap

The word antinomian means "against the law." Study 2 introduced it as one of the two ditches that border the road of genuine Christian experience. Here we take a longer look at one particular form of it — not the philosophical position that Christians have no obligations, but the far more common practical pattern: using grace as a ceiling.

The grace-as-ceiling person is not usually someone who has thought through their position. They have simply absorbed a version of the gospel that emphasizes only one side: God loves me, my sins are forgiven, I am accepted. They have heard these truths repeatedly, and they believe them. What they have not fully integrated is what those truths imply about the life they are now living. They experience forgiveness as a reset button rather than a rescue. They return to the same confession regularly without particular distress, because they have learned that forgiveness follows. They have confused the patience of God with his indifference.

Jude 4 is a startling verse. People who "pervert the grace of our God into sensuality" are not simply the notoriously immoral. The word Jude uses for "pervert" means to transpose, to change something into something it is not. The grace of God — the rescue from sin — has been transposed into permission for sin. Forgiveness has become license. The same grace that was meant to free them from sin is being used to enable it.

Cheap Grace

Dietrich Bonhoeffer, writing from within Nazi Germany in the 1930s while watching the German church capitulate around him, gave this error its most memorable name: "cheap grace." He defined it in The Cost of Discipleship as the preaching of forgiveness without requiring repentance, baptism without church discipline, absolution without personal confession. Grace without the cross. Grace without Jesus Christ, living and incarnate.

Bonhoeffer was writing about a specific historical situation, but he identified something perennial. Cheap grace is grace received without being transformed by it. It is the blessings of the gospel without the claims of the gospel — the pardon without the changed allegiance, the relationship without the relationship's demands. It is what happens when "I am forgiven" is experienced as the end of the story rather than the beginning of a new one.

How It Works in Practice

The grace-as-ceiling pattern rarely announces itself as theology. It operates as a set of habits and instincts. Some of the most telling signs:

"I've already asked forgiveness for this." Used before the sin rather than after it, this phrase reveals a transactional view of the relationship — as if the confession has been pre-filed and the account is ready for the next withdrawal. There is no sense that the sin itself is a problem beyond its legal status. The relationship is intact by paperwork.

The absence of any plan to fight. The grace-as-ceiling Christian may confess sin regularly without ever asking the follow-on question: what is the Spirit doing in me to change this? They receive forgiveness but have not enrolled in the life of mortification — the active, Spirit-empowered putting to death of the sinful habits. They are at peace with the gap between confession and change because they have decided, without quite articulating it, that change is not really what is being asked.

Grace-language that does no work. "I'm just resting in grace." "I trust God with this." These phrases may be describing real faith and real rest. But they can also be describing an arrangement in which the hard work of genuine repentance — the turning, the fighting, the killing of sin before it kills us — has been labeled "not trusting grace" and quietly set aside. If the grace-language in your vocabulary never makes demands on you, it may have been domesticated past the point of recognition.

What Grace Actually Does

Romans 6 is Paul's answer to the question he raised in verse 1, and it is worth reading slowly. He does not say: well, technically you could keep sinning, but it would be ungrateful. He says: how can we who died to sin still live in it? The question is not about appropriateness. It is about possibility. It is about what has actually happened.

"We know that our old self was crucified with him in order that the body of sin might be brought to nothing, so that we would no longer be enslaved to sin. For one who has died has been set free from sin."

Romans 6:6–7 (ESV)

The grace of the gospel is not merely forgiveness — though it is certainly that. It is union with Christ in his death and resurrection. The person who has been united to Christ has died, really died, to sin's dominion. The power of sin over them has been broken — not removed from their environment, not made unavailable to tempt them, but broken as a ruling principle. They are no longer sin's slaves. They are its rebels.

Titus 2:11–12 captures the same truth from the other direction: "For the grace of God has appeared, bringing salvation for all people, training us to renounce ungodliness and worldly passions, and to live self-controlled, upright, and godly lives in the present age." Grace trains. It is not passive. It is not a pardon sitting on a shelf; it is an active principle, working against ungodliness from the inside.

If the grace you have received has not been training you — if it has produced no war against sin, no growing hatred of what you once loved, no increasing desire for holiness — it is worth asking whether you have received the real thing or a substitute.

The Ferguson Lens

The antinomian error is the right ditch. It takes the genuine truth — that our standing with God does not depend on our obedience — and draws the wrong conclusion: that our obedience therefore does not matter. What it has missed is that the God who justifies us also sanctifies us; that his grace rescues us from sin's penalty and from sin's power; that Christ came not only to bear the punishment of our sin but to deliver us from the pattern of it. Using grace as a ceiling is not a sophisticated misunderstanding of an obscure doctrine. It is a refusal to believe that God cares what you do with the life he has redeemed.

Christ, Our Anchor

Romans 6:5–11 is both the diagnosis and the cure. "For if we have been united with him in a death like his, we shall certainly be united with him in a resurrection like his." The grace-as-ceiling person has stopped the story at forgiveness. The resurrection has not yet entered the picture. But forgiveness is not the last word of the gospel; resurrection is.

The resurrection of Jesus is not only a past historical event. Paul says we are united with it — that Christ's resurrection life flows into those who belong to him, so that we are genuinely new creatures, genuinely changed, genuinely free from the dominion of what once owned us. This is the reason Paul can say "by no means!" to the suggestion of continuing in sin. It is not merely inappropriate for those who are forgiven. It is inappropriate for those who are alive — who have been raised with Christ into a different kind of life entirely.

If the gospel of forgiveness has not yet become the gospel of resurrection for you — if you are living as if you have received a pardon but not a new life — then you are living below what grace has actually given you.

  1. The study describes confession as a "reset button" rather than a genuine turning. Have you ever caught yourself in that pattern? What does it feel like when confession has become routine rather than genuine?
  2. Bonhoeffer said cheap grace is grace without the cross, grace without Jesus Christ living and incarnate. What would "expensive" grace — real grace — actually require of the person who receives it?
  3. The grace-language signs ("I'm resting in grace," "I trust God with this") can describe either genuine faith or avoidance. How do you tell the difference in your own use of these phrases?
  4. Titus 2:11–12 says grace "trains us." In what specific ways has grace been training you — changing your desires, your habits, your instincts over time? Are there areas where it seems not to be doing that? What might that say?
  5. Romans 6 says we are "dead to sin" — that its dominion has been broken. Does that feel true in your experience? If not, is the gap between the doctrine and your experience something to explore with God?
