The Watchman's Warning

A Study in Self-Examination, Honest Reckoning, and the Mercy That Calls Us Home

"Son of man, I have made you a watchman for the house of Israel. Whenever you hear a word from my mouth, you shall give them warning from me."

Ezekiel 3:17 (ESV)

This study is not a comfortable one. It was not meant to be. It was written — and it comes to you — with something closer to grief than accusation, with a lump in the throat rather than a pointed finger.

Most of us who call ourselves Christians have a long and practiced relationship with the parts of the faith we find encouraging. We return to the promises. We hold fast to grace. We treasure the truths that steady us. And we should. But somewhere along the way, many of us have learned to quietly step around the parts that are harder — the warnings Scripture will not let us sleep through, the sins we have agreed not to look at too closely, the questions we have silently agreed not to ask.

This study exists to look at those places. Not to condemn. Not to earn God's favor through spiritual severity. But because the same God who speaks peace to the troubled heart also sounds a trumpet over the comfortable one. Because a love that never warns is not love at all. And because — and this is where we begin and end — there is genuine hope on the other side of honest reckoning.

The title comes from Ezekiel 33. God appoints the prophet as a watchman for Israel. If the watchman sees the sword coming and does not blow the trumpet, and someone dies, their blood is on the watchman's hands. But if the watchman warns and the person does not listen, the watchman is clear. God uses this image to describe his own heart toward the wicked: "As I live, declares the Lord God, I have no pleasure in the death of the wicked, but that the wicked turn from his way and live. Turn back, turn back from your evil ways, for why will you die?" (Ezekiel 33:11).

That cry — why will you die? — is the spirit of this entire study. It is not the voice of a judge reading a sentence. It is the voice of a father watching a child walk toward a cliff. The trumpet blast is not an end; it is an invitation to turn. Every warning in this study is meant to be heard in that tone.

How to Use This Study

Each study is designed for a small group meeting, though many have found it equally useful for personal reflection. The studies build on one another, but most can stand alone. At the end of each page you will find collapsible discussion questions — these can be opened for group use or left closed for individual reading.

We recommend reading the Pastoral Guide (Study 0) before beginning, whether you are a group leader or reading alone. It frames how to approach these subjects with both honesty and care.

Throughout the study, you will encounter a recurring diagnostic lens drawn from Sinclair Ferguson's work. It helps us recognize the two most common ways Christians drift from the truth — and more importantly, what lies underneath both drifts. Study 2 introduces it; subsequent studies apply it.

Every study ends with a Christological anchor — a specific facet of who Jesus is or what he has done that speaks directly into the subject at hand. This is by design. The warnings are real. So is the mercy. We want to end every study looking at him.

A Word Before You Begin

Jonathan Edwards preached "Sinners in the Hands of an Angry God" — the sermon most often associated with fire and terror — without dramatic gestures. By many accounts, he held his manuscript close and read quietly. People wept. Not because he was performing alarm, but because they sensed that what he was saying was true, and that he was saying it because he cared whether they lived or died.

That is the spirit we reach for here. If any page in this study stings, we hope it stings the way a surgeon's work stings — not to harm, but to heal. And we hope that you will find, as you go, that the same God who sounds the trumpet is the one who opens the gate.
