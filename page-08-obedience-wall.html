Study 8

When Obedience Becomes a Wall

Striving that substitutes for trusting

"Come to me, all who labor and are heavy laden, and I will give you rest. Take my yoke upon you, and learn from me, for I am gentle and lowly in heart, and you will find rest for your souls. For my yoke is easy, and my burden is light."

Matthew 11:28–30 (ESV)

"O foolish Galatians! Who has bewitched you? It was before your eyes that Jesus Christ was publicly portrayed as crucified. Let me ask you only this: Did you receive the Spirit by works of the law or by hearing with faith? Are you so foolish? Having begun by the Spirit, are you now being perfected by the flesh?"

Galatians 3:1–3 (ESV)

This is the other ditch — the one that looks more like seriousness about God than its mirror image does. And it is, in its way, just as lethal.

The Portrait of a Legalist

The legalist, as we are using the term here, is not necessarily someone who believes they are earning salvation. They may have an impeccable Reformed theology on justification. What defines them is not their stated doctrine but the emotional and functional grammar of their spiritual life: everything depends on performance. Not on God's performance — on theirs.

They are serious people. They read their Bible. They attend church faithfully. They care about holiness — genuinely care, not performatively. They feel the weight of sin more acutely than most around them. And yet something is wrong, and they can feel it, even if they cannot name it. Their spiritual life is exhausting. Prayer feels more like a report than a conversation. Every sin produces not only remorse but a kind of spiritual score-keeping, a running tally of how they are doing relative to where they think they should be. The joy of the Lord, which they have been taught is their strength, is conspicuously absent most of the time.

In Luke 18, Jesus tells the parable of the Pharisee and the tax collector praying in the temple. The Pharisee's prayer is stunning in its transparency: "God, I thank you that I am not like other men, extortioners, unjust, adulterers, or even like this tax collector. I fast twice a week; I give tithes of all that I get" (Luke 18:11–12). He is not lying. He probably does fast twice a week. He probably does tithe. His statement of fact is accurate. What is wrong is not the facts but the function — what those facts are doing in his soul. They are establishing a position. They are building a wall between him and the people he considers less spiritually accomplished. And they are, most dangerously, substituting for the one thing the tax collector had that he did not: a genuine sense of his need.

Two Faces of Legalism's Failure

Paul asks the Galatians: having begun by the Spirit, are you now being perfected by the flesh? The question assumes that what got you here is not what will complete you. That the energy that fueled the beginning — grace, received in helplessness — cannot be replaced by the energy you generate yourself, however genuine your effort.

Legalism fails in one of two ways, and they are both instructive.

When the Legalist Thinks They Are Succeeding

If performance is the standard and the performance is going reasonably well, the result is a particular kind of pride — the Pharisee's pride. It produces hardness toward those who are not performing as well. It produces impatience with messiness, with failure, with the complexity of real lives not lived at the same level of apparent spiritual discipline. It produces a kind of gospel that is, in practice, reserved for people like themselves: those who have shown they are serious, who have done the work, who deserve the blessing. The scandal of grace — that it is given to the ungodly — becomes quietly intolerable.

When the Legalist Knows They Are Failing

If performance is the standard and the performance is not going well — and for most legalists, the more honest they are, the more they feel this — the result is a different but equally destructive outcome: despair, resentment, or the slow abandonment of trying. Because the legalist has built their relationship with God on what they produce, any honest self-assessment will ultimately come up short. There is no mercy built into the system. There is only the performance and the verdict.

This is why Study 6 noted that spiritual exhaustion and drift are often the final destination of failed legalism. The person who has been striving in their own strength for years, and who has found it unbearable and unrewarding, who has never discovered that the Christian life was never meant to be lived on those terms — that person often ends up either in proud religiosity or in the antinomian ditch, having traded one form of self-reliance for another.

The Weight Jesus Addresses

Matthew 11:28 is addressed, explicitly, to those who "labor and are heavy laden." This is not spiritual language for everyone who is tired. It is specific language for those who are exhausted by the burden of a religion driven by performance. The "yoke" of first-century Jewish piety that Jesus's audience would have understood was the yoke of the law as a system of earning — the accumulated weight of trying to be good enough by trying hard enough.

Jesus does not say the burden is a misunderstanding that he will now correct theologically. He says he will give them rest. Something changes when someone comes to him. Not the standards — Jesus is not offering lower moral expectations. But the basis — the ground on which you stand — shifts entirely. You are no longer standing on your performance. You are standing on his. The yoke you now bear is his yoke: not the weight of earning, but the glad obedience of someone who has been forgiven and received and is now learning from one who is "gentle and lowly in heart."

The Ferguson Lens

The person who swings from legalism to antinomianism — who was once very strict and is now very loose — has not solved the underlying problem. They have traded one form of self-trust for another. The legalist trusted their own obedience. The antinomian trusts their own freedom. Neither has arrived at the place of trusting God — trusting that his grace is real, that his commands are good, that his Spirit is at work, and that the obedience he calls for will be, in time, provided. The solution to the exhaustion of legalism is not the ease of antinomianism. It is the discovery that genuine obedience, flowing from a heart that trusts God, is not exhausting at all. It is the yoke that is easy and the burden that is light.

A Word to the Serious

This study is not an argument against seriousness about holiness. It is an argument against the wrong kind of seriousness. God does care what you do. The law does make real demands. The call to mortify sin is genuine (Colossians 3:5). The difference is not whether you are serious, but what the seriousness is resting on.

If your seriousness about holiness produces weariness, chronic self-condemnation, and a growing suspicion that God is mostly disappointed in you, it is resting on the wrong foundation. It is producing Pharisee-fruit. The mark of genuinely gospel-shaped obedience is not that it is painless — the putting to death of sin is work — but that it flows from love, from gratitude, from the security of being already accepted, rather than from the anxiety of trying to become so.

"We love because he first loved us" (1 John 4:19). That sequence matters. The love is not what earns the first love. The first love is what makes our love possible at all.

Christ, Our Anchor

Matthew 11:28–30 is the anchor for this study because it speaks directly into the exhaustion of performance-based religion. But it is worth noticing the exact terms of the invitation: "Learn from me, for I am gentle and lowly in heart." The one who calls the weary to rest is not primarily giving them a doctrine to believe but a person to know — a person whose character is gentleness and lowliness. The resting point is not a correct understanding of justification (though that is precious); it is a personal relationship with the one who says, learn from me.

This is why legalism is ultimately a relational failure as much as a theological one. It has gotten the doctrine half-right — it knows God requires something — but it has missed who he is. It has not yet arrived at the knowledge that the one who requires everything has also given everything, and that his demands now come not as a threat to the unaccepted but as an invitation from the one who has already accepted you. Knowing that changes everything about how you obey.

  1. The study says the legalist may have impeccable Reformed theology on justification but still live by performance in practice. Does that distinction — between stated doctrine and functional grammar — resonate with your experience?
  2. The Pharisee in Luke 18 is not lying about his deeds, but something has gone wrong in what those deeds are doing in his soul. What is that something? Can you see it in yourself?
  3. The study describes two failure modes: the proud legalist who is apparently succeeding, and the despairing legalist who knows they are not. Which feels more familiar, or more frightening?
  4. Jesus says, "learn from me, for I am gentle and lowly in heart." What would it mean to know God as someone who is genuinely gentle — not indifferent, but gentle? Does that characterization of God feel true to your experience of him?
  5. The study says that obedience flowing from love is "not exhausting at all." Is that your experience of Christian living? If not, what do you think is driving your obedience — love, fear, habit, or something else?
