Study 11

Assurance, Fruit, and Where to Look

The most pastorally complex question in this study

"Therefore, since we are surrounded by so great a cloud of witnesses, let us also lay aside every weight, and sin which clings so closely, and let us run with endurance the race that is set before us, looking to Jesus, the founder and perfecter of our faith."

Hebrews 12:1–2 (ESV)

"By this we shall know that we are of the truth and reassure our heart before him; for whenever our heart condemns us, God is greater than our heart, and he knows everything."

1 John 3:19–20 (ESV)

Here is the most pastorally dangerous moment in this entire study: we have spent ten pages examining the reality of sin, drift, hardening, and spiritual danger. Now we must address the question everyone is already asking beneath the surface: so where do I actually stand? And how do I know?

Why This Is Genuinely Hard

The question of assurance — the question of whether you are genuinely saved, truly in Christ, really held — is not a simple one, and anyone who treats it as simple is either fortunate in their circumstances or is not paying close enough attention. The reason it is complex is not that Scripture is evasive about it. It is that the same symptom can mean opposite things, depending on what is underneath.

Consider grief over sin. A person who is grieved by their sin, who finds it difficult to rest in grace, who keeps returning to confess the same things — this could be the sign of a genuinely tender conscience, the Spirit pressing against the heart's stubborn places, the mark of someone who is genuinely, if incompletely, alive to God. Or it could be a form of perfectionist despair, a wounded pride that cannot bear to be wrong, a religious neurosis that mistakes self-punishment for repentance. The symptom is identical. The diagnoses are opposite.

Or consider peace about sin. A person who feels settled, who rarely experiences sharp conviction, who reads the warnings in this study and does not feel particularly addressed by them — this could be genuine, Spirit-wrought assurance, the fruit of a long, honest walk with God who has brought them through much and settled them in peace. Or it could be the comfortable numbness of a conscience that has been slowly hardened over years. Again: same symptom, opposite diagnoses.

This is why quick pastoral answers in this area are often worse than no answers at all. The reassurance that fails to distinguish between these cases can leave the person in despair with false comfort, or leave the person in complacency undisturbed. The alarm that fails to distinguish can break a tender conscience without serving it.

What the Westminster Confession Says

The Westminster Confession of Faith, Chapter XVIII, is one of the most carefully crafted treatments of assurance in the Protestant tradition. It says that assurance is grounded in three things: the divine truth of the promises of salvation, the inward evidences of those graces to which those promises are made, and the testimony of the Spirit of adoption.

The order matters. The primary ground is the divine truth of the promises — not our assessment of our spiritual condition, but the objective, external word of God that says: those who believe are accepted, and the God who says it does not lie. This is the foundation. It is not inside us. It is in Christ, testified in Scripture, sealed by his blood.

The "inward evidences" — what we sometimes call fruit — come second. They are real. They are confirmatory. They matter. But they are confirmatory evidence, not foundation. You do not build your assurance on them the way you build it on the promise. You allow them to confirm the promise, to put flesh on it, to show you that it is alive in you. But the moment you invert the order — the moment your confidence in Christ depends on first reaching a satisfactory assessment of your fruit — you have built on shifting ground, and the first serious look at your actual spiritual life will shake it.

Looking at Christ, Not at Your Look

Tim Keller, in his short book The Freedom of Self-Forgetfulness, draws on Paul's remarkable statement in 1 Corinthians 4:3–4: "But with me it is a very small thing that I should be judged by you or by any human court. In fact, I do not even judge myself. For I am not aware of anything against myself, but I am not thereby acquitted. It is the Lord who judges me."

Paul is not here modeling spiritual carelessness. He is not saying self-examination is unimportant. He is saying something subtler and more liberating: his standing does not rest on his verdict about himself. He has transferred the judgment entirely to God. And because of what Christ has done, that transfer produces not anxiety but freedom — the freedom of someone who knows they are held by something far more reliable than their own assessment of their condition.

The goal of assurance, then, is not a confident positive self-assessment — an inner verdict that says, "yes, I pass." The goal is freedom from preoccupation with the question altogether. Not because the question is unimportant, but because it has been answered in a place outside yourself, and you can rest there.

Hebrews 12:1–2 gives the practical form of this: "looking to Jesus, the founder and perfecter of our faith." Not looking to your looking. Not assessing the quality of your gaze. Looking to the one who is both the origin and the completion of your faith — the one in whom it began and the one in whom it will finish. When you look to him, you find someone who is not confused about your standing, not changing his mind, not surprised by the things you are most ashamed of, and not waiting for you to get your act together before he holds you.

What Fruit Is For

Having said all of this, the role of fruit should not be minimized. The same letter that says "God is greater than our heart" (1 John 3:20) also says, earlier in that section: "By this we shall know that we are of the truth" — and what precedes is a description of genuine, active love for brothers and sisters (1 John 3:18). John does not think fruit is irrelevant to assurance. He thinks it is confirming evidence.

2 Corinthians 13:5 says: "Examine yourselves, to see whether you are in the faith. Test yourselves." This is a genuine call. Self-examination has its place. It belongs in the Christian life. The question is what it is examining for, and what it does with what it finds.

Genuine fruit — love, repentance, desire for God, hunger for the Word, grief over sin, movement toward holiness over time, even if not in a straight line — these are signs that something real has happened. They are not the same as perfection. They are not the same as the absence of struggle. They are the marks of a life that is genuinely, if imperfectly, oriented toward God. And a life genuinely oriented toward God can take those marks as confirming evidence of what the promises already say.

The person who has no such marks at all — who finds no desire for God, no grief over sin, no movement in the direction of holiness over any significant period of time — is the person who should sit most seriously with the questions this study has been asking. Not in despair, but in honest reckoning.

The Ferguson Lens

Assurance is where both ditches produce their most visible damage. The legalist cannot rest in assurance because they are always aware that their performance is insufficient — and since performance is what they believe their standing rests on, insufficient performance means insufficient standing. The antinomian may have a superficial "assurance" that is actually presumption — a settled confidence that they are fine, which no longer connects to any self-examination at all. Genuine assurance is neither the legalist's anxious striving nor the antinomian's comfortable assumption. It is the settled rest of someone who has looked honestly at themselves, found themselves lacking, and then looked to Christ and found him fully sufficient.

Christ, Our Anchor

Romans 5:8–10 is one of the strongest assurance passages in all of Scripture, and it is built entirely on what Christ has already done: "but God shows his love for us in that while we were still sinners, Christ died for us. Since, therefore, we have now been justified by his blood, much more shall we be saved by him from the wrath of God. For if while we were enemies we were reconciled to God by the death of his Son, much more, now that we are reconciled, shall we be saved by his life."

The argument moves from greatest to least: if God loved you when you were his enemy, how much more will he hold you now that you are his child? If Christ died for you when you were in full rebellion, how much more will he keep you now that you have been received? The foundation of your assurance is not the current state of your spiritual performance. It is the love of God expressed in the cross, when you deserved none of it. That love does not depend on what you are producing. It depends on what Christ produced, once, fully, finally, for you.

Look to Jesus. Keep looking. He is the founder and the perfecter. He does not begin a work and abandon it. The one who started the good work in you will bring it to completion (Philippians 1:6).

  1. The study says that grief over sin and peace about sin can both be signs of spiritual health or spiritual danger, depending on what is underneath. How do you discern which is which — in yourself? Is there someone in your life who could help you discern?
  2. The Westminster Confession grounds assurance first in God's promises, second in fruit. Is that the order in which you actually approach assurance? If not, how do you approach it, and what does that produce in you?
  3. Keller's observation about Paul (1 Corinthians 4) is that Paul does not even judge himself — his standing rests on God's verdict, not his own. How does it feel to give up the right to render a verdict on yourself? Is that freedom, or does it feel unsafe?
  4. Hebrews 12:1–2 says "looking to Jesus." What is the difference between looking to Jesus and looking to your look at Jesus — scrutinizing the quality of your own faith? What makes the second one so tempting?
  5. Romans 5:8–10 says Christ died for us "while we were still sinners" and "enemies." How does that truth change the way you approach assurance — and how does it change the way you think about others who are still in that condition?
