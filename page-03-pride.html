Study 3

Pride: The Parent Sin

The sin that thinks it is not sinning

"Pride goes before destruction, and a haughty spirit before a fall."

Proverbs 16:18 (ESV)

"Do nothing from selfish ambition or conceit, but in humility count others more significant than yourselves."

Philippians 2:3 (ESV)

There is a particular cruelty in beginning a study on pride: the first reaction of a proud person to a warning about pride is often to think of someone else who needs it more. If you are already doing that, you have just confirmed the study's central point. Welcome. We are all here.

C.S. Lewis and the Great Sin

C.S. Lewis devoted a chapter of Mere Christianity to pride. He called it "The Great Sin" — not one among many, but the one from which most of the others draw their energy. Lewis made an observation that has rightly become famous: pride is essentially competitive. It is not the pleasure of having something, he wrote, but the pleasure of having more than the next person. A person who is proud of being rich does not simply enjoy their wealth — they enjoy it more when they compare it to someone who has less. Remove the comparison, and the pleasure vanishes.

This is why pride is so spiritually devastating. It is not merely a character flaw in the way that impatience or shyness might be. It is a fundamental orientation of the self — one that puts me at the center and measures everything in relation to that center. The proud person does not primarily want good things. They want to be better than. They want to be seen. They want to win.

And here is the bitter irony that Lewis also named: because pride is fundamentally about comparison, the proudest people in a religious community are often those most confident in their own virtue. The person who has worked hardest on their humility can end up prouder of their humility than less spiritual people are of their achievements. The Pharisee in Luke 18 is not a caricature; he is a warning to everyone who reads the passage and thinks, at least I am not like him.

John Owen and the Last Sin to Die

John Owen, the great Puritan theologian, spent his life watching sin at work — in his congregation, in the culture, and most relentlessly, in himself. One of his most sobering observations was this: pride is among the last sins to die in a believer's heart. Other sins may be overcome earlier in the Christian life — blatant immorality, obvious cruelty, visible vices. But pride — particularly the spiritual pride that attaches itself to progress in holiness — tends to remain long after its companions have been routed. It is like a weed that goes underground when you spray the surface, continuing its quiet work where you cannot see it.

Owen wrote extensively about spiritual pride, the particular form of the disease that afflicts people who take their faith seriously. The very acts of devotion that should produce humility — regular prayer, Scripture reading, careful attendance to one's spiritual condition — can become, in the proud heart, a new source of comparison and self-congratulation. I am more disciplined than them. I have gone deeper than they have. I see things they miss. The medicine becomes the poison.

What Pride Actually Does

Pride does not merely produce arrogance, though it does that. More deeply, pride creates blindness. The proud person cannot see themselves accurately, because seeing yourself accurately requires the willingness to be less than you thought — and pride will not allow that. This is why Proverbs 16:18 connects pride not merely to embarrassment but to destruction: the proud person walks toward the cliff because they are too proud to believe the warnings they see. The fool in Proverbs is not someone of low intelligence; he is someone whose pride has disconnected him from reality.

Pride also makes us unteachable. The student who is convinced they already know makes a poor student. The believer who is already confident in their spiritual maturity will not press into the places where they are actually most broken. Pride is therefore not simply a problem in itself; it is the guardian of all the other problems, the one that keeps the door closed against self-examination.

And pride is deeply relational. It damages our closest relationships, because the proud person cannot truly apologize (an apology requires admitting you were wrong), cannot genuinely serve (service requires putting someone else first), and cannot receive correction (correction requires acknowledging that someone else sees something you missed). Pride is the engine of most serious relational breakdowns, and it is the spiritual glue that holds them in place long after they should have been resolved.

The Most Dangerous Form: Spiritual Pride

Of all its forms, spiritual pride deserves the most attention in a study like this. It is the most invisible, the most resistant to rebuke, and the most damaging in a community of faith.

Spiritual pride says: I have suffered more, and therefore I understand grace better. It says: I am more theologically precise, and therefore my faith is more genuine. It says: I have been through the dark night of the soul, and that puts me ahead of those who haven't. It even says, in its most refined form: I know I am a sinner — and that knowledge of my sinfulness is itself a sign of my spiritual depth.

Notice what is happening in each case: genuine spiritual insight (grace is real, theology matters, suffering teaches, self-knowledge is valuable) is being used not to draw closer to God but to establish distance from other people. The insight itself is not the problem. What pride does is attach itself to the insight and convert it into a measuring rod.

Edwards, who wrote a treatise on distinguishing genuine religious affections from counterfeit ones, identified spiritual pride as one of the most reliable signs that a religious experience is not from God. Not the only sign, and not infallible — but a pattern worth noting: whatever produces genuine work in the heart tends to produce humility. Whatever produces pride, however spiritually decorated, is suspect. The measuring rod is itself the evidence.

The Ferguson Lens

Pride shows up differently in the two ditches. The legalist tends to wear their pride openly — in the form of comparing their spiritual performance favorably against others. It is Pharisee-pride: I fast twice a week, I give a tenth. The antinomian tends to wear it more quietly — a pride in their freedom, in their refusal to be bound by rules, in the sophistication of their theology of grace. Both are forms of self-elevation. Both are resistant to the same cure: genuine encounter with the God who is infinitely higher than our best performance and infinitely more gracious than our most expansive theology.

The Person Who Does Not Read This Study

Perhaps the most useful thing to notice about pride is that the people who most need this study are the least likely to recognize themselves in it. This is by design — pride is, by its nature, self-concealing. You cannot see clearly with the very faculty that is distorted.

This is not a dead end. It is an invitation to prayer. We cannot diagnose our own pride with confidence, because the proud heart will manage the diagnosis in its favor. But God, who searches hearts, can show us what we cannot show ourselves. "Search me, O God, and know my heart! Try me and know my thoughts! And see if there be any grievous way in me, and lead me in the way everlasting!" (Psalm 139:23–24). That is not a dangerous prayer. It is the most clarifying one available to us.

Christ, Our Anchor

Philippians 2 is the great Christological antidote to pride. Paul writes: "Have this mind among yourselves, which is yours in Christ Jesus, who, though he was in the form of God, did not count equality with God a thing to be grasped, but emptied himself, by taking the form of a servant, being born in the likeness of men. And being found in human form, he humbled himself by becoming obedient to the point of death, even death on a cross" (Philippians 2:5–8).

Jesus had, literally, infinite reason for pride. He is the one before whom Lewis's competitive pride would dissolve immediately — there is no comparison to be made; there is no one higher. And yet this is the one who took the form of a servant. This is the one who washed the feet of the disciples who would abandon him hours later. This is the one who, on the cross, in agony, prayed for those who nailed him there.

We will never cure our pride by trying harder to be humble. But we can, by the Spirit's work, gaze long and steadily at the one who had every reason to be proud and was not — and find, over time, that something in us begins to change. The cure for pride is not self-improvement. It is beholding Christ.

  1. Lewis says pride is essentially competitive — it requires comparison. Can you identify an area of your life where this is true for you? Where do you tend to measure yourself against others?
  2. Owen says pride is one of the last sins to die in a believer. Does that match your experience? Where have you seen spiritual growth apparently produce spiritual pride rather than humility?
  3. The study describes "spiritual pride" — using genuine spiritual insight as a measuring rod against others. Have you experienced this, either in yourself or directed at you? What does it feel like from both sides?
  4. The study says you cannot reliably diagnose your own pride, and suggests prayer as the response. How does it sit with you to ask God to show you what you cannot see in yourself? Is there resistance to that prayer? What does the resistance say?
  5. Philippians 2 holds up Christ's humility as both example and transformation. What is the difference between trying harder to be humble and "beholding Christ" until humility grows? What would the second approach look like in practice?
