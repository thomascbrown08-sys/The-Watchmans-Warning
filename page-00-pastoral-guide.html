Before You Begin

Pastoral Guide

For group leaders and those reading alone

What follows is not a study to be administered. It is a study to be inhabited. A note to those who will lead it — and to those who will sit with it alone.

For the Group Leader

You are about to guide people through territory that is genuinely dangerous to cross carelessly. These studies probe the places where Christians become most defensive, most fragile, and most in need of both honesty and gentleness at the same time. This is not a reason to avoid that territory. It is a reason to enter it with care.

A few things are worth keeping in mind as you prepare.

This study will land differently on different people

In any group of ten people working through this material, you are almost certainly looking at a wide spread of spiritual conditions. One person is drifting and doesn't know it. Another is in the pit of despair and already knows it too well. One has constructed elaborate theological reasons why the harder parts of these studies don't apply to them. Another is terrified they have already crossed a line they cannot uncross. One has been carrying real guilt for years and simply needs permission to believe that forgiveness is real. Another is quite comfortable in their sin and needs to be shaken loose.

The same words will hit these people very differently. The passage that finally breaks through a hard heart may crush an already tender one. This is not a reason to soften every edge. But it is a reason to know your group, to watch faces, and to be available after the meeting ends.

Pat answers are not pastoral care

One of the recurring themes of this study — particularly in the studies on assurance and serious warnings — is that the same symptom can have opposite diagnoses. Grief over sin can be a sign of genuine, Spirit-worked repentance. It can also be a form of perfectionist despair that has nothing to do with faith and everything to do with wounded pride. Someone who seems comfortable can be resting in genuine assurance. Or they may be hardening. These are not the same, and they do not get the same response.

When group members share something that fits these categories, resist the pull toward the quick reassurance ("you're fine, God loves you") and also toward the quick alarm ("you need to examine yourself more closely"). Neither is always right. The better posture is curiosity: ask what they mean. Ask what is underneath it. Sit with the complexity a moment before you reach for the remedy.

Pastoral Discernment Note

A rule of thumb from the Reformed pastoral tradition: the person who is terrified they have grieved God beyond recovery almost always has not. That terror is itself the Spirit's work. The person who is not troubled at all is the one who warrants greater concern. Use that asymmetry as a quiet guide when you are uncertain.

On the study about the unpardonable sin

Study 10 requires the most care of any study in this collection. The subject — the blasphemy of the Holy Spirit — is one that produces genuine fear in some believers, occasionally to the point of crisis. Please read that study in full before leading it. Be ready with the reassurance built into the study itself: the person who fears they have committed this sin is almost certainly not the person it describes. That reassurance is not minimizing the passage; it is the direct implication of what the passage actually says.

If a group member becomes genuinely distressed during this study, it is appropriate to pause, address them directly with pastoral care, and potentially follow up privately. This study is not worth someone leaving in worse condition than they arrived.

On Studies 7 and 8 (the two ditches)

These studies — on antinomianism and legalism — are mirror images of each other, and they tend to provoke different reactions from different personality types. The person who resonates strongly with one study and dismisses the other probably needs the other one. If your group nods vigorously through Study 7 (on cheap grace) and gets defensive during Study 8 (on performance-based religion), or vice versa, that asymmetry is itself worth naming in discussion.

On leading discussion

The discussion questions at the end of each study are designed to be honest without being intrusive. They invite personal application but do not require it. As a leader, model the kind of honest self-examination the studies are calling for — not theatrical confession, but genuine acknowledgment that the material applies to you too. If you lead from behind a wall of apparent invulnerability, the group will follow suit.

Do not rush the silence. Some of the most important moments in a small group study happen in the thirty seconds after a hard question, when no one has spoken yet. Sit in it. Let people think.

For the Individual Reader

If you are working through this study on your own — without a group, perhaps in a season of private examination — this guide has something to say to you as well.

First: the fact that you are here is a good sign. The person who is hardened does not typically seek out material like this. The willingness to sit with uncomfortable spiritual questions is itself a mark of the Spirit's work. Do not despise it.

Second: solitary reading of convicting material can be hard to calibrate. Without other people in the room, it is easier to do two unhelpful things. One is to breeze past the hard parts by reading quickly and never pausing to apply anything personally. The other is to sit with the hard parts too long and turn conviction into despair. Neither serves you well.

A suggestion: read each study at a deliberate pace. When something lands — when a passage or a sentence makes you want to skip ahead, or makes your chest tighten — stop there. That is likely the place that most needs your attention. Sit with the Scripture it is drawn from. Bring it to God in prayer before you read on.

Third: if at any point in this study you find yourself in genuine crisis — if the material about serious warnings or the unpardonable sin has produced real fear or despair that will not lift — please do not navigate that alone. Bring it to a pastor, a mature Christian friend, a trusted mentor. This study is meant to produce honest self-examination, not to leave you isolated in darkness. The watchman sounds the trumpet; he does not abandon the city.

A Word on Pace

This study is not a sprint. Each page is designed to take roughly fifteen minutes to read, but the material calls for far more than reading. A single study, chewed on slowly for a week, will do more good than all twelve read in a sitting. Give the Word time to do its work.

The Spirit in Which This Was Written

This study was written with one conviction firmly in place: God does not warn us because he is eager to condemn us. He warns us because he is determined to save us.

Ezekiel 33:11 is the hinge on which this entire study swings: "As I live, declares the Lord God, I have no pleasure in the death of the wicked, but that the wicked turn from his way and live." The trumpet blast in that chapter is not the sound of condemnation being executed. It is the sound of condemnation being refused — God refusing to let his people walk into destruction without a word, because his desire is always that they would turn and live.

Every warning in this study is meant to be heard in that key. We are not here to frighten people into submission, or to make believers feel permanently unsafe, or to cast doubt where Scripture offers assurance. We are here because the same love that says "you are accepted" also says "do not go back to what was killing you." Both are acts of the same grace.

Lead, and read, accordingly.
